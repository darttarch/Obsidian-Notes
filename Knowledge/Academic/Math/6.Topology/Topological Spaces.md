>[!def]
>Let $X$ be a set, then $\mathcal{T}$, a collection of subsets of $X$ ($\mathcal{T}\subset\mathcal{P}(X)$ ) is called a **topology** on $X$, if $\mathcal{T}$ satisfies the following conditions:
>
>$1.$ $X$ and $\varnothing$ are elements of $\mathcal{T}$.
>
>$2.$ Any arbitrary unions of elements of $\mathcal{T}$ is in $\mathcal{T}$.
>
>$3.$ Any finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{T}$ is in $\mathcal{T}$.

>[!example]
>Let $X$ be a set.
>The collection $\mathcal{T}_1=\set{\varnothing, X}$ is a topology in $X$, and it's called the *indiscrete* topology.
>
>The collection $\mathcal{T}_2=\mathcal{P}(X)$ is also a topology on $X$ and it's called the *discrete* topology.


>[!def]
Let $\mathcal{T}$ and $\mathcal{T'}$ be two topologies on $X$.
If $\mathcal{T}'\subset\mathcal{T}$ we say that $\mathcal{T}$ is **finer** than $\mathcal{T'}$. If $\mathcal{T}'$ is a proper subset of $\mathcal{T}$, then we say $\mathcal{T}$ is **strictly finer** than $\mathcal{T'}$.
We say **coarser** or **strictly coarser** in the respective cases.
